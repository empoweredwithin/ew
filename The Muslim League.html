The Muslim League - EW

Questions:

  1. Write the factors responsible for the formation of Muslim league.
  2. What did the Muslims demand The Viceroy Lord Minto?
  3. What are the events that led to the formation of Muslim league?
  4. What were the aims and objectives of Muslim league?
  5. Write the significance of Lucknow Pact,1916.
  6. What is the Lucknow pact? Write any three clauses of the Lucknow Pact.
  7. Write the drawbacks of the Lucknow Pact.

Answers:

  1. The factors responsible for the formation of Muslim league are:

    • Muslims became bitter enemies of the British after they established their supremacy in India after dethroning Mughal rulers, who were the followers of Islam.
    • The British policy of 'divide and rule' affected the Muslims and increased their hatred towards Hindus.
    • Muslims were relatively backward and were in a disadvantage.
    • Negative influence of Principal Beck on Sir Sayyid Ahmed Khan, who said, that Anglo-Muslim unity was possible but Hindu-Muslim unity was not possible.
    • The manner in which Indian history was interpreted in those days contributed to the growth of communal thinking among the Hindus and Muslims.

  2. Muslims demanded:

    • Representation of Muslims in elected bodies on the basis of their political importance and not on their numerical strength
    • Separate electoral constituencies for the Muslims in the provincial council and the Imperial Legislative Council.
    • Preference to be given to Muslims while nominating members of the Viceroy's Council.
    • Reservation of seats for Muslims in the state services.
The Muslim League (Continued)

  1. The events that led to the formation of Muslim league are:

    • The MAO Association was formed in 1893 to promote support for the British and to prevent Muslims from participating in any political movement.
    • Partition of Bengal in 1905 created a wedge between the Hindus and Muslims.
    • Lord Minto in 1906 assured Muslims that their political rights would be safeguarded.
    • The Mohammedan Educational Conference was held at Dhaka, and it accepted the proposal to form a central organization for the Muslims.

  2. The objectives of league were laid down as follows:

    • To promote, among the Muslims of India, support for the British government.
    • To safeguard the political rights and interests of the Muslims.
    • To prevent rise of feelings of hostility between the Muslims of India and other communities.
    • To present the needs and aspirations of the Muslims to the government.

  3. Significance of Lucknow Pact 1916:

    • The join scheme symbolized Hindu-Muslim unity. The Congress compromised and accepted the scheme of separate electorates for Muslims, and The Muslim league accepted the principles of elections and the majority rule.
    • The split that happened between the early and the assertive nationalists in the year 1907, was again tied up in 1916. As a result, Congress was strengthened.
    • Since the Hindu-Muslim and the early-assertive nationalists' bond was strengthened, it put a lot of pressure on the government.
    • The government therefore felt it necessary to pacify the Indians by promising gradual development of self-government institutions in India.
The Muslim League (Concluded)

  1. The Lucknow Pact was an agreement reached in 1916 between the Indian National Congress (INC) and the All-India Muslim League. It marked a significant moment in India's struggle for independence, as it brought the two major political groups together to present a united front against British colonial rule.

    • The India Council in London, which controlled Indian affairs, was to be abolished.
    • The Pact introduced separate electorates for Muslims
    • The Pact provided for adequate and separate representation of Muslims in governance.
    • Separation of judiciary from the executive.

  2. The drawbacks of the Lucknow Pact are as follows:

    • It did not involve the masses.
    • It emphasized the separate political existence for Hindus and Muslims.
    • It did not resolve the deadlock between the executive and the legislatures.
    • It provided for a 'communal veto' in legislation.
    • The government's repressive policy, followed by the declaration of August 1917.
